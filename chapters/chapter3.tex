\chapter{Methodology}%
\label{chapter:methodology}

\begin{introduction}
This chapter presents the methodology adopted to design, implement, and validate the modular co-simulation framework proposed in this dissertation. It describes the research approach, the architectural decisions that guided the system design, the co-simulation infrastructure, and the simulation scenarios developed to demonstrate and evaluate the framework's capabilities across energy, mobility, and telecommunications domains.
\end{introduction}

\section{Research Approach}

This work follows a \textbf{Design Science Research} (DSR) methodology, a paradigm suited to applied engineering research where the primary goal is to create and evaluate a purposeful artifact that solves a clearly defined problem. DSR is particularly appropriate for this dissertation because the central objective is not merely to observe or explain phenomena, but to \textit{build} a technically sound simulation framework capable of addressing the cross-domain modeling limitations identified in Chapter~\ref{chapter:introduction}.

The research process is organized into four iterative phases:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Problem Identification and Motivation}: The limitations of existing domain-specific simulation tools and the absence of modular, reusable cross-domain architectures were identified through the literature review presented in Chapter 2. The motivation is grounded in the needs of urban management platforms such as Ubiwhere's UBP, which requires simulation capabilities spanning energy, mobility, and telecom domains.

    \item \textbf{Architecture Design}: A modular simulation architecture was designed with a clear separation of concerns between a domain-agnostic simulation engine and domain-specific modules. This design followed well-established software engineering principles including separation of concerns, interface segregation, and inversion of control.

    \item \textbf{Framework Development and Scenario Implementation}: The proposed architecture was implemented as a functional prototype in Python. Domain modules for energy systems and urban mobility were developed, and concrete simulation scenarios were built to exercise each domain independently and---through HELICS co-simulation---in combination.

    \item \textbf{Empirical Validation}: The implemented scenarios were executed and their outputs compared against known benchmarks and expected behaviors. Within-domain metrics (voltage stability, charging costs, travel times, emissions) were used to validate correctness, while the co-simulation layer was validated by verifying time synchronization and message exchange across federates.
\end{enumerate}


\section{System Architecture}

The proposed framework follows a three-layer modular architecture designed to achieve a strict separation between generic simulation infrastructure and domain-specific logic. This structural decision is central to the thesis objective of building a reusable simulation engine that can accommodate multiple urban domains without coupling. \subsection{Core Simulation Engine}

The lowest layer is a \textbf{domain-independent simulation engine} responsible for all generic simulation concerns: agent lifecycle management, event scheduling, temporal stepping, and state propagation. It makes no assumptions about the nature of the entities being simulated---whether they are power grid components, vehicles, or network nodes---relying instead on well-defined interfaces.

The engine supports the following core abstractions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Agents}: Autonomous entities with an internal state and a \texttt{step()} function invoked at each simulation tick. Agents encapsulate all domain-specific behavior and are entirely managed by the engine.
    \item \textbf{Time Control}: Discrete-event temporal stepping with configurable resolution. Each scenario defines its own $\Delta t$, ranging from one second (Scenario M1) to fifteen minutes (Scenario E1).
    \item \textbf{Contracts and Interfaces}: Each domain module must implement a defined interface including initialization, per-step update, and reporting methods. This contract ensures that new domains can be integrated without modifying engine internals.
    \item \textbf{Collector}: A generic metrics accumulation mechanism decoupled from any domain-specific reporting logic.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Domain Modules}

The middle layer consists of \textbf{autonomous domain modules}, each encapsulating the logic, models, and agents of one urban infrastructure domain:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Energy Module}: Models electrical distribution networks including generation assets (solar PV, wind turbines), distributed storage (batteries), and responsive loads (Scenarios E1 and E2).
    \item \textbf{Mobility Module}: Agent-based urban traffic simulation with vehicles, signalized intersections, A*-based routing, and adaptive signal control algorithms (Scenario M1).
    \item \textbf{Telecommunications Module} (planned): Will simulate 5G network slicing and dense IoT deployments in urban environments (Scenarios T1 and T2).
\end{itemize}

Each module is independently runnable in standalone mode or as a HELICS federate. Only the \texttt{use\_helics} flag differs between the two modes; domain logic is unchanged.

\subsection{Co-Simulation Layer}

The top layer is the \textbf{co-simulation orchestration layer}, built on HELICS (Hierarchical Engine for Large-scale Infrastructure Co-Simulation)~\cite{helics}. HELICS manages:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Federate Registration}: Publications and subscriptions for each domain module.
    \item \textbf{Time Synchronization}: A grant-request protocol ensuring all federates advance in step. A federate requests time $t + \Delta t$; HELICS grants it only when no messages at earlier times are pending from other federates.
    \item \textbf{Data Exchange}: State variables (e.g., EV power demand from the mobility module consumed by the energy module) flow through HELICS value federates, avoiding direct API coupling.
    \item \textbf{Broker}: A single broker process coordinates up to three federates; the architecture is designed to scale to additional domains without structural changes.
\end{itemize}


\section{Design Decisions}

\subsection{Technology Selection}

\subsubsection{Co-Simulation Middleware: HELICS}

HELICS~\cite{helics} was selected over Mosaik~\cite{mosaik_cosim} and FMI/FMU~\cite{fmi_standard} for the following reasons:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multi-Domain Design}: HELICS was explicitly designed for large-scale multi-domain infrastructure simulation, whereas Mosaik has stronger roots in smart grid scenarios~\cite{mosaik_smartgrid}.
    \item \textbf{Time Management Flexibility}: HELICS supports both time-stepped and event-driven federates, accommodating the different time resolutions of the energy (15 min), mobility (1 s), and telecom (event-driven) domains.
    \item \textbf{Demonstrated Scalability}: HELICS has been validated in scenarios with hundreds of federates~\cite{helics_transactive}, and in energy-transport co-simulation specifically~\cite{helics_ev_transport}.
\end{itemize}

FMI was found less suitable due to its primary focus on mechanical and embedded systems, with limited native support for agent-based or network-level models~\cite{fmi_application}.

\subsubsection{Implementation Language: Python}

Python was selected because of its first-class HELICS bindings (\texttt{helics} package), the \texttt{numpy} scientific computing ecosystem for numerical models, rapid prototyping capability suited to a research context, and alignment with Ubiwhere's existing data platform stack.

\subsection{Architectural Patterns}

\subsubsection{Separation of Concerns}

Domain agents must implement \texttt{step()}, \texttt{initialize()}, and \texttt{generate\_report()} according to the engine contract, but have no access to engine internals. This prevents domain logic from polluting the generic engine and ensures each module can be tested independently.

\subsubsection{Federation Pattern}

In co-simulation mode, each domain module registers its inputs (subscriptions) and outputs (publications) with HELICS at initialization, then participates in a synchronized time loop. Modules communicate exclusively through HELICS topics, providing publish-subscribe decoupling: adding a new domain requires only registering new topics, not modifying existing modules.

\subsubsection{Strategy Pattern for Scenario Variants}

Within Scenarios E2 and M1, multiple behavioral variants are implemented using the strategy pattern. In Scenario E2, the \texttt{ChargingStrategy} enum selects between \texttt{UNCOORDINATED}, \texttt{SMART}, and \texttt{V2G} behaviors at runtime. In Scenario M1, the \texttt{adaptive\_signals} flag switches between fixed-time and queue-adaptive signal control. This enables comparative evaluation without code duplication in the core simulation loop.

\subsection{Energy Domain Scenarios}

\subsubsection{Scenario E1: Smart Grid with Renewable Integration}

Scenario E1 simulates an IEEE 33-bus radial distribution network under high renewable penetration, combining solar PV, wind generation, battery storage, and demand-responsive loads~\cite{smartgrid_challenges, renewable_integration}. The scenario covers a 24-hour period at 15-minute resolution (96 time steps).

\paragraph{Component Models}

\textbf{Solar PV}: Fifty installations (5--10 kW each) follow a sine-based irradiance model during daylight hours (06:00--18:00):
\begin{equation}
P_{solar}(t) = P_{rated} \cdot \sin\!\left(\frac{(h-6)\,\pi}{12}\right) \cdot C_{cloud} \cdot \eta_{sys}
\label{eq:solar_power}
\end{equation}
where $h$ is the hour of the day, $C_{cloud} \sim U(0.8, 1.0)$ is a stochastic cloud factor, and $\eta_{sys} = 0.85$.

\textbf{Wind Turbines}: Three 500 kW turbines follow a three-region power curve:
\begin{equation}
P_{wind}(v) =
\begin{cases}
0 & v < v_{ci} \\[4pt]
P_{rated} \cdot \!\left(\dfrac{v - v_{ci}}{v_{r} - v_{ci}}\right)^{\!3} & v_{ci} \leq v < v_{r} \\[4pt]
P_{rated} & v_{r} \leq v < v_{co} \\[4pt]
0 & v \geq v_{co}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:wind_power}
\end{equation}
with cut-in $v_{ci} = 3.0\,\text{m/s}$, rated $v_{r} = 12.0\,\text{m/s}$, and cut-out $v_{co} = 25.0\,\text{m/s}$.

\textbf{Battery Energy Storage (BESS)}: Five 50 kWh systems (25 kW max, 0.5C) operate within $SOC \in [0.20,\,0.95]$. The SOC update during charging is:
\begin{equation}
SOC_{new} = SOC_{current} + \frac{P_{charge} \cdot \Delta t \cdot \eta_{charge}}{E_{capacity}}
\label{eq:soc_charge}
\end{equation}
with $\eta_{charge} = 0.92$, giving a round-trip efficiency of $\approx 84.6\%$.

\textbf{Residential Loads}: 800 households with time-varying load factors (0.5 off-peak to 1.0 during evening peak 17:00--22:00) and $\pm 10\%$ stochastic variation. Demand Response (DR) activates for 70\% of loads during peak hours, reducing consumption by 15\%~\cite{der_challenges}.

\paragraph{Power Balance and Curtailment}

Net system power at each time step:
\begin{equation}
P_{net}(t) = P_{load}^{total}(t) - P_{renewable}(t)
\end{equation}
Batteries charge when $P_{net} < 0$ (surplus) and discharge when $P_{net} > 0$ (deficit). Curtailment occurs when batteries are full and surplus remains:
\begin{equation}
P_{curtail}(t) = \max\!\left(0,\; -P_{net}^{final}(t)\right)
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Voltage Model}

Bus voltages are estimated via a simplified linear drop model:
\begin{equation}
V_{bus}(i) = V_{base} - k_{drop} \cdot P_{bus}(i) \cdot i
\end{equation}
Violations are counted against ANSI C84.1 limits ($V \notin [0.95,\, 1.05]$ p.u.).

\subsubsection{Scenario E2: Electric Vehicle Charging Infrastructure}

Scenario E2 models the impact of 100 EVs on a modified IEEE 13-node distribution feeder with three charging strategies~\cite{der_challenges}. Nodes 1--8 host 20 Level 2 charging stations (four 7.2\,kW ports each, 80 residential chargers total); nodes 9--13 host 5 DC fast stations (two 50\,kW ports each). The grid feeder capacity is 2500\,kW with a 2000\,kW base load at peak (load factor 1.0 during 17:00--22:00). Time resolution is 5 minutes; the simulation runs for 36 hours (432 steps) to cover overnight residential charging through next-morning departure.

\paragraph{Arrival Patterns}

Residential EVs (80\% of fleet): $t_{arrival} \sim \mathcal{N}(18.5, 1.0)\,\text{h}$; depart next morning at $t_{dep} \sim \mathcal{N}(8.0, 0.5)\,\text{h}$. Commercial EVs (20\%): $t_{arrival} \sim U(8,18)\,\text{h}$ with 1--2 hour dwell time at DC fast stations.

\paragraph{Time-of-Use Tariff}

\begin{equation}
c(h) = \begin{cases}
0.08\;\text{USD/kWh} & 0 \leq h < 7 \;\text{or}\; 23 \leq h < 24 \\
0.20\;\text{USD/kWh} & 17 \leq h < 21 \\
0.12\;\text{USD/kWh} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:tou}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Charging Strategies}

\textbf{Uncoordinated}: Each vehicle charges at full rated power immediately upon connection, with no grid awareness. This baseline typically produces a sharp demand peak coinciding with the residential evening peak.

\textbf{Smart}: A priority-based algorithm allocates available grid capacity to vehicles sorted by an urgency score:
\begin{equation}
U_i(t) = \frac{E_{need,i}(t)}{T_{available,i}(t)}
\label{eq:urgency}
\end{equation}
combined with a price factor ($F_{price} = 0.5$ during on-peak, 1.0 otherwise), ensuring vehicles with less time and more remaining energy need are served first.

\textbf{V2G}: Extends smart charging with bidirectional power flow. V2G-capable vehicles (50\% of fleet, $P_{discharge}^{max} = 10\,\text{kW}$) export energy to the grid during on-peak hours (17:00--21:00) whenever their SOC exceeds the minimum threshold, and recharge during off-peak hours. V2G revenue is compensated at a 20\% premium over the current TOU rate.

\subsection{Mobility Domain Scenarios}

\subsubsection{Scenario M1: Urban Traffic Congestion Management}

Scenario M1 simulates a $5 \times 5\,\text{km}$ urban road grid with 2,500 agent-based vehicles and 25 signalized intersections (1250\,m spacing), comparing fixed-time and adaptive signal control strategies on travel time, congestion, and CO$_2$ emissions~\cite{mobility_challenges, agent_traffic_overview}. Vehicles depart stochastically over the first hour ($t_{dep} \sim U(0, 3600)\,\text{s}$) and traverse each link at 50\,km/h ($\approx$\,90\,s per link). The simulation runs for 3 hours at 1-second resolution (10,800 steps).

\paragraph{Routing: A* Algorithm}

Vehicle routes are computed using A* with a Manhattan distance heuristic:
\begin{equation}
f(n) = g(n) + h(n), \quad h(n) = |x_n - x_{goal}| + |y_n - y_{goal}|
\label{eq:astar}
\end{equation}
Alternative routes are generated stochastically using neighbor weights $w_n = 1/(d_n + 1) + \xi$, $\xi \sim U(0, 0.3)$, to distribute traffic across the network.

\paragraph{Adaptive Traffic Signal Control}

Each intersection cycles between two phases—North–South~(NS) and East–West~(EW)—separated by a fixed 3-second yellow interval. At every phase transition the controller counts the vehicles waiting at the intersection, classifying each one as NS or EW demand according to the direction of its next route segment. This directional queue measurement is the key input to the adaptive algorithm.

In adaptive mode, green time is allocated proportionally to the measured demand:
\begin{equation}
T_{green} = T_{min} + (T_{max} - T_{min}) \cdot \frac{Q_{phase}}{Q_{NS} + Q_{EW}}
\label{eq:adaptive_signal}
\end{equation}
with $T_{min} = 15\,\text{s}$ and $T_{max} = 90\,\text{s}$. When one direction is heavily loaded (e.g.\ $Q_{NS} \gg Q_{EW}$), that phase receives a green time close to $T_{max}$, while the lightly loaded phase receives only $T_{min}$. Under equal demand the two phases share the available green time equally, replicating fixed-time behaviour as a special case.

In fixed-time mode, the controller ignores the measured queues and assigns equal weight to both phases ($Q_{NS} = Q_{EW} = 1$), yielding a constant cycle length regardless of traffic conditions. This provides a baseline against which the adaptive strategy is compared.

\paragraph{Emissions Model}

CO$_2$ emissions are computed using a two-state model:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E_{CO_2} =
\begin{cases}
\alpha_{idle} \cdot \Delta t & v < 1.0\,\text{m/s} \\
\alpha_{move} \cdot v \cdot \Delta t & v \geq 1.0\,\text{m/s}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:emissions}
\end{equation}
with $\alpha_{idle} = 2.31\,\text{g/s}$ and $\alpha_{move} = 0.15\,\text{g/m}$.

\subsection{Planned Scenarios}

\textbf{Scenario M2: Autonomous Vehicle Integration} will simulate a 10 km highway/arterial with 1,000 vehicles at CAV penetration rates of 0\%, 25\%, 50\%, and 75\%, evaluating cooperative adaptive cruise control (CACC), platooning efficiency, and time-to-collision safety metrics.

\textbf{Scenario T1: 5G Network Slicing} will simulate 7 gNBs serving 500 mobile users across three slices (eMBB, URLLC, mMTC), evaluating slice isolation, handover performance, and QoS for heterogeneous smart city applications~\cite{telecom_challenges}.

\textbf{Scenario T2: Dense IoT Network} will evaluate NB-IoT and LTE-M performance under 5,000 IoT devices in a 2 km$^2$ industrial park, focusing on congestion during synchronized reporting periods and the impact of edge computing offloading.

\subsection{Cross-Domain Integration}

The HELICS-based architecture enables coupled cross-domain simulations. Three integration scenarios are defined as future work:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{E2 + M1 (Energy--Mobility)}: EVs navigate through traffic to available charging stations; charging demand feeds back into the grid model~\cite{helics_ev_transport}.
    \item \textbf{M2 + T1 (Mobility--Telecommunications)}: Evaluates how 5G network latency and handover events affect autonomous vehicle control loops.
    \item \textbf{E1 + T2 (Energy--Telecommunications)}: Evaluates how IoT communication reliability affects smart grid monitoring and demand response activation.
\end{itemize}


\section{Evaluation Methodology}

The framework is evaluated along two dimensions: the correctness of domain models and the effectiveness of the modular architecture.

\subsection{Domain Model Validation}

Each implemented scenario is validated against expected physical behaviors:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{E1}: Voltage profiles must vary within realistic bounds for the IEEE 33-bus system; curtailment must occur only when battery storage is saturated (capacity or charge-rate limited); DR must produce a measurable load reduction between 17:00 and 22:00.
    \item \textbf{E2}: Smart charging must reduce peak demand compared to uncoordinated; V2G must decrease net charging costs; all vehicles in non-V2G strategies must meet their departure SOC target ($\geq$\,0.8). V2G vehicles may trade departure SOC for grid revenue, with $\geq$\,95\% meeting the target.
    \item \textbf{M1}: Adaptive control must reduce average travel time and total delay; emissions must decrease in proportion to reduced idling; queue lengths must be lower than under fixed-time control.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Architectural Validation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Domain Independence}: Each module executes identically in standalone and co-simulation modes; only \texttt{use\_helics} differs.
    \item \textbf{Interface Compliance}: Verified by confirming all domain agents implement the engine contract without modifying engine internals.
    \item \textbf{Strategy Substitutability}: Strategy variants (E2, M1) produce statistically distinct outcomes under identical inputs, confirming correct behavioral switching.
    \item \textbf{Time Synchronization}: In co-simulation mode, HELICS time grants are verified to ensure all federates advance in step and cross-domain data arrives before use.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary}

This chapter described the Design Science Research methodology underpinning the dissertation, the three-layer modular architecture of the proposed co-simulation framework, and the rationale for key design and technology decisions. Three simulation scenarios---E1 (smart grid with renewables), E2 (EV charging infrastructure), and M1 (urban traffic management)---were presented with their mathematical models, component descriptions, and evaluation criteria. Three additional scenarios and cross-domain integration cases were defined as the planned scope for completing the framework.

The next chapter presents the implementation details of the framework prototype and the results obtained from executing the implemented scenarios.
