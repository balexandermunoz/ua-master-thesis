[Slide 3]
Modern cities depend on three infrastructure networks: energy, mobility, and telecommunications. They are not independent— electric vehicles link power grids and traffic, smart traffic needs telecom, and energy depends on communication networks for coordination.

The problem is that today's simulation tools work in isolation. Each one handles a single domain very well, but none of them can model interactions across domains. There are some platforms that are great at monitoring and aggregating data, but they can't answer predictive cross-domain questions — things like "what happens to the power grid when 500 EVs charge for one hour?" or "how does closing a road affect both traffic and the energy network?"

Our goal is to build a flexible, domain-agnostic simulation engine that supports multi-domain co-simulation and can answer exactly those kinds of what-if questions.

[Slide 5]
We adopted Design Science Research, which is convenient when the goal is to build and evaluate an engineering artifact rather than just observe a phenomena. The process has four phases. First, we identify the problem: existing simulation tools are domain-specific and there's no modular, reusable architecture for cross-domain urban simulation. Second, we design a three-layer architecture. Third, we implement the framework in Python with domain modules and concrete scenarios. Fourth, we validated the outputs against expected physical behaviors and known benchmarks. These phases are iterative — feedback to refinement.


[Slide 6]
Here you can see the overall architecture. It follows three layers. The main goal is to have a separation between the generic logic and the domain logic.
At the bottom, a domain-agnostic core engine handles all generic simulation concerns. In the middle, independent domain modules encapsulate the logic for energy, mobility, and telecom. At the top, HELICS orchestrates cross-domain co-simulation.

This design is specifically tailored for integration with Ubiwhere's Urban Business Platform. The pub-sub architecture and modular interfaces mean that simulation outputs can directly feed into UBP's data pipelines without custom adapters. This alignment ensures that research prototypes can transition into production platform capabilities with minimal redesign.



[Slide 7]
The core engine provides four functionalities. Agents are autonomous entities with a step function called every tick (Battery, a car...). Time control supports configurable resolution from 100 milliseconds up to 15 minutes. The engine defines a contract: every domain module must implement initialize, step, and generate_report. And a generic collector accumulates metrics without knowing what they represent. This means adding a new domain — say water or waste management — requires no changes to the engine. You just implement the contract and plug in.


[Slide 8]
We built three domain modules. The energy module models distribution networks with solar panels, wind turbines, battery storage, and demand-responsive loads. The mobility module is an agent-based traffic simulation with A-star routing and adaptive signal control. The telecom module simulates 5G network slicing with resource allocation across multiple stations. Crucially, each module runs identically in standalone mode or as a HELICS federate.
Mathematics is the language used to abstract the domain concepts into agents, generating realistic data and enabling different domains to communicate with each other.


[Slide 9]
Helics supports both time-stepped and event-driven federates, which is essential because our energy module runs at 15-minute steps while telecom runs at 100-millisecond events. HELICS manages federate registration, time synchronization, and data exchange through value federates. And adding a new domain means registering new topics, not modifying existing modules.



[Slide 10]
We implemented six scenarios. E1 and E2 cover the energy domain — smart grid with renewables and EV charging infrastructure. M1 covers urban traffic. T1 covers 5G network slicing. E2+M1 couples energy and mobility, while M1+T1 couples mobility and telecommunications. Time steps range from 100 milliseconds in telecom to 15 minutes in the grid scenario. All six scenarios support both standalone and co-simulation modes, enabling comparative evaluation of domain isolation versus integrated cross-domain effects.


[Slide 11]
Evaluation has two dimensions. For domain correctness, each scenario has specific physical expectations — voltages within IEEE bounds, smart charging reducing peak demand, adaptive signals reducing travel time, dynamic slicing improving utilization, and coupling producing measurably different outcomes than isolation. 
For architectural validation, we verify that modules behave identically in standalone and co-simulation modes, that all agents comply with the engine contract, that strategy variants produce statistically distinct results, and that HELICS time grants ensure proper synchronization.

[Slide 13]
Now let's speak about the technical challenges we had to solve during implementation.
First, scalability in mobility loops: with millions of agent steps, we needed to optimized where possible, mainly by computing each vehicle route once and reusing it.
Second, cross-domain coordinate alignment: different modules use different spatial representations, so we introduced a mapping layer to couple them consistently.

The plot on this slide gives a simple view of why the scalability decision works.
On the left, execution time grows almost linearly as the number of vehicles increases, which is what we expect from this design and confirms it remains manageable at 2,500 vehicles.

On the right, we see something interesting: average queue length actually decreases at higher volumes. Showing that regardless the amount of cars, we can generate useful insights

So the key message is: we kept the model scalable and stable without breaking modularity between domains — and the data shows that adaptive control actually improves with scale.

Quick note on T1 terminology (5G Slicing):
• eMBB = Enhanced Mobile Broadband: high-speed internet, video streaming — needs 2 resource blocks
• URLLC = Ultra-Reliable Low-Latency Communication: autonomous vehicles, remote surgery — also needs 2 RBs but is failure-intolerant
• mMTC = Massive Machine-Type Communication: IoT sensors, smart meters, intermittent traffic — needs only 1 RB
• RB (Resource Block) = smallest unit of 5G spectrum; we have 100 total per base station
• Static slicing: reserves 50% for eMBB, 30% for URLLC, 20% for mMTC — regardless of actual demand
• Dynamic slicing: allocates RBs based on real-time usage — improves overall utilization but creates winners and losers
The key insight is that dynamic allocation helps broadband users (+479% QoS) but hurts IoT devices (-44.4%), which is why operators need fairness constraints in production systems.

[Slide 17]
This slide is about the limitations, and I want to be clear: these are not accidental shortcomings — they are self-imposed design choices made deliberately to keep the prototype simpler and more focused.
In the energy case, the voltage model is simplified to linear, avoiding the complexity of AC power-flow solvers while still capturing grid behavior for our scenarios.
In mobility, routes are computed once at departure rather than continuously rerouted, which keeps computation tractable for 2,500 vehicles.
And in telecom, we use an abstracted radio model instead of full packet-level simulation to maintain clarity in the cross-domain integration.
These trade-offs are intentional and justified because the goal was to prove the three-layer architecture works for multi-domain coupling, not to be a production simulator.
The critical point is: the architecture itself is not limited. Each of these simplifications can be lifted independently — better voltage solvers, dynamic routing, detailed radio models — without requiring structural changes. That's the power of the modular design.

This slide focuses on BaseFederate, the core abstraction of the engine.
Conceptually, it defines a clean contract with three abstract methods: initialize components, run the simulation loop, and generate the report.
Every domain module implements that same contract, which gives us consistency across energy, mobility, telecom, and cross-domain scenarios.

At the same time, BaseFederate provides reusable concrete behavior, especially HELICS setup and time advancement.
That means domain code does not need to include co-simulation boilerplate.
Another practical benefit is dual-mode execution: the same module can run standalone or with HELICS by changing a flag, not by rewriting logic.
This is one of the main reasons the framework remains modular and easy to extend.

[Slide 27]
Scenario E1 models an IEEE 33-bus distribution network with 50 solar panels, 3 wind turbines, 
5 battery units, and 800 residential loads with demand response. We validate voltage profiles, 
curtailment behavior, and DR load reduction. Scenario E2 puts 100 EVs on a 13-node feeder and compares 
three charging strategies: uncoordinated, where everyone charges immediately; smart, 
which prioritizes by urgency — energy needed divided by time available; and V2G, which adds bidirectional 
power flow during peak hours at a 20% premium. We use the strategy pattern so all three variants share 
the same simulation loop with no code duplication.

[Slide 28]
M1 simulates 2,500 vehicles on a 5-by-5 kilometer grid with 25 signalized intersections. Vehicles route using A-star with a Manhattan heuristic plus random perturbation for route diversity. We compare fixed-time signals, which give equal green to both directions, against adaptive signals, which allocate green time proportionally to the measured queue at each phase change. The formula is simple: green time scales linearly between 15 and 90 seconds based on the ratio of directional demand. We also track CO2 emissions with a two-state model — idle emissions at 2.3 grams per second and moving emissions at 0.15 grams per meter.

[Slide 29]
T1 simulates three 5G base stations serving 200 mobile users across three network slices: eMBB for broadband, URLLC for low-latency, and mMTC for IoT devices. We use the 3GPP path loss model with shadow fading, random waypoint mobility, and a handover mechanism with hysteresis and cooldown to prevent ping-pong effects. We compare static slicing, which fixes resource block allocation at 50-30-20 percent, against dynamic slicing, which redistributes proportionally to instantaneous demand. This is conceptually analogous to M1's adaptive traffic signals — both allocate capacity based on real-time demand rather than fixed assumptions.

[Slide 30]
This is the key integration scenario. Five hundred EVs share the road network with 2,000 background vehicles. Thirteen charging stations provide 52 ports. We compare two modes under the same random seed. In coupled mode, EVs physically drive through traffic, wait at red lights, and cause congestion. In uncoupled mode, EVs teleport to their station — treating energy and mobility as independent domains. The multi-rate alignment runs traffic at 1-second ticks and charging logic every 5 minutes. We measure three coupling-specific metrics: average EV drive time, which should be zero uncoupled but significant coupled; emission uplift from EV driving; and SOC target deficit, since travel delays reduce available charging time.
